\title{Closed-form Second-order Data Shapley as a Spurious-Correlation Attenuator:\\
Where it Works and Where it Doesn't}
\author{Anonymous Submission}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We adapt the closed-form In-Run Data Shapley of \citet{wang2024inrun} into an
\emph{in-training} per-sample reweighter for worst-group robustness. Our
second-order variant augments the gradient inner product with an RMS-rescaled
gradient--Hessian--gradient cross-term. We \emph{decompose} this cross-term and,
through a residual ablation, show that its benefit comes from an orthogonal,
sample-specific residual rather than from mere smoothing
(real $>$ shuffle $>$ parallel; Corrupted-CIFAR $+5.9$~pp, $p=0.0015$). The
mechanism reproduces across \textbf{three} from-scratch spurious regimes ---
color (Colored MNIST), texture (Corrupted-CIFAR), and natural images
(Spurious-STL10) --- where, among methods that use \emph{no group labels at
training time}, the second-order reweighter beats vanilla SGD and a corrected
bi-level meta-learner (on STL10, $+28.1$/$+17.2$~pp over vanilla/Ren2018 and a
$+12.9$~pp second-order isolation over the first-order term; all $p<0.011$,
10 seeds). Stronger methods exist (JTT, GroupDRO); we do \emph{not} claim
state-of-the-art. On pretrained-ResNet Waterbirds fine-tuning all our variants
fail to match the bi-level baseline, and we characterize this regime boundary.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Models trained by empirical risk minimization latch onto \emph{spurious cues}
--- background, color, texture --- that correlate with the label in training but
shift or flip at test time, so average accuracy stays high while the
\emph{worst group} collapses \citep{sagawa2020groupdro}. The strongest remedies
require group labels at training (GroupDRO) or multi-stage / bi-level pipelines
(JTT, \citealp{liu2021jtt}; meta-reweighting, \citealp{ren2018reweight}).

We ask whether a \emph{closed-form, single-pass} per-sample reweighter --- no
group labels in the model update, no inner optimization loop --- can attenuate
the spurious cue. Our starting point is In-Run Data Shapley
\citep{wang2024inrun}, which gives a closed-form Taylor estimate of each
sample's contribution to a validation anchor \emph{during} training. We
repurpose this valuation as a training-time weight, and ask what the
\emph{second-order} curvature term adds beyond the first-order inner product.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
\item We repurpose closed-form In-Run Data Shapley as an in-training per-sample
reweighter for worst-group robustness (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
\item A second-order gradient--Hessian--gradient cross-term with \textbf{RMS
rescaling}, which is required for stability under strong spurious bias.
\item A \textbf{decomposition and residual ablation} establishing that the
cross-term's gain is a real, sample-specific directional signal --- the
orthogonal residual --- not global smoothing (real $>$ shuffle $>$ parallel;
sign-flip collapses).
\item Evidence that the mechanism \textbf{reproduces across three distinct
spurious regimes} (color, texture, natural images), with a statistically
isolated second-order benefit in each.
\item An \textbf{honest regime boundary}: a clean negative on pretrained-ResNet
fine-tuning, with a mechanistic diagnosis and a practical recommendation.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Data valuation.}
Data Shapley \citep{ghorbani2019datashapley} values training points by their
marginal contribution but needs many retrainings; In-Run Data Shapley
\citep{wang2024inrun} makes this closed-form within a single run. We repurpose
that estimator --- not for post-hoc valuation but as a training-time reweighter
--- and add a curvature term.

\paragraph{Reweighting for robustness and fairness.}
Learning to reweight \citep{ren2018reweight} and FORML \citep{yan2022forml}
solve a bi-level meta problem; ARL \citep{lahoti2020arl} reweights adversarially
without demographics. Our reweighter is closed-form and single-pass, with no
inner optimization loop.

\paragraph{Worst-group robustness.}
GroupDRO \citep{sagawa2020groupdro} uses group labels; JTT \citep{liu2021jtt}
and IRM \citep{arjovsky2019irm} relax that requirement. These are stronger but
heavier; we position Fairds as a lightweight closed-form alternative and report
the gap honestly.

\paragraph{Shapley for fairness.}
FairShap \citep{arnaiz2023fairshap} uses Shapley values for fair data valuation;
we instead inject a \emph{second-order} Shapley signal into the optimization
trajectory itself.

\section{Method: Fairds}
\label{sec:method}

\paragraph{Setup.}
At step $t$ with parameters $\theta_t$, a minibatch $\mathcal{B}=\{z_i\}_{i=1}^B$
and a small \emph{group-balanced anchor} $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{val}}$ (50:50,
constructed once; the model update consumes no sensitive labels). Let
$g_i=\nabla_\theta L(z_i;\theta_t)$ and
$g_{\mathrm{val}}=\nabla_\theta L(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{val}};\theta_t)$.

\paragraph{First order (Fairds-1).}
$\phi_i^{(1)}=\langle g_i,g_{\mathrm{val}}\rangle$ is the Wang et al.\ estimator
of how much $z_i$ reduces validation loss in one SGD step. Per-sample gradients
are obtained with \texttt{torch.func.vmap} in a single autograd graph --- closed
form, no bi-level loop.

\paragraph{Second order (Fairds-2) with RMS rescaling.}
The second-order term adds the gradient--Hessian--gradient cross-term
$c_i=\langle g_i, H_{\mathrm{val}}\,g_{\mathrm{val}}\rangle$, where
$H_{\mathrm{val}}\,g_{\mathrm{val}}$ is computed by the Pearlmutter trick
\citep{pearlmutter1994hvp} (one extra backward pass, no explicit Hessian). The
spectrum of $H_{\mathrm{val}}$ varies by orders of magnitude during training, so
a fixed $\alpha$ is unusable. We \textbf{rescale the cross-term to the same
root-mean-square magnitude as the first-order term} within each minibatch:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde\phi_i^{(2)}
= \phi_i^{(1)} - \alpha\,
\frac{\mathrm{RMS}(\phi^{(1)})}{\mathrm{RMS}(c)}\; c_i .
\label{eq:phi2}
\end{equation}
This rescaling is an algorithmic contribution: without it, second-order Fairds
collapses to chance accuracy under strong spurious bias.

\paragraph{Pipeline.}
We (1) compute $g_i$ and (for second order) $H_{\mathrm{val}}g_{\mathrm{val}}$;
(2) update an exponential moving average $\mathrm{EMA}_i$ with
$\widetilde\phi_i$; (3) form per-batch weights
$w_i=B\cdot\mathrm{softmax}(\mathrm{EMA}_{\mathcal{B}}/\tau)$, clipped at quantile
bounds; and (4) take a weighted SGD step
$\theta_{t+1}=\theta_t-\eta\sum_i w_i\nabla_\theta L(z_i;\theta_t)$. For models
with batch normalization we freeze BN running statistics so vmap per-sample
gradients stay coupling-free.

\paragraph{What does the cross-term do?}
We find $g_{\mathrm{val}}$ is nearly the top eigenvector of $H_{\mathrm{val}}$
(alignment $0.89$--$0.99$), so the normalized cross-term $c_n$ is almost
parallel to $\phi^{(1)}$. Decomposing
$c_n=\beta\,\phi^{(1)}+r$ separates a \emph{parallel} part (a curvature
shrinkage, equivalent to global smoothing) from an \emph{orthogonal residual}
$r$. The empirical question is whether $r$ is a real signal; we answer it in
Section~\ref{sec:residual}.

\section{Experiments}

We compare, on OOD worst-group test accuracy (the spurious cue is flipped at
test time): Vanilla SGD; our Fairds-1/2 and the residual variant
$\phi^{(1)}-\alpha r$; a corrected one-step bi-level meta-learner
(\citealp{ren2018reweight}, a FORML proxy); JTT \citep{liu2021jtt}; and GroupDRO
\citep{sagawa2020groupdro}. Vanilla, Fairds, Ren2018 and JTT use \emph{no group
labels} in the update; GroupDRO uses them. Each method sees a small balanced
anchor for its inner computation only.

\subsection{Three spurious regimes}
\label{sec:regimes}

We use three from-scratch benchmarks with the same anchor / held-out
\texttt{val\_eval} / OOD-test protocol, differing only in the spurious modality:
\textbf{Colored MNIST} (color; \citealp{arjovsky2019irm}-style),
\textbf{Corrupted-CIFAR} (texture: Gaussian blur vs.\ additive noise on
car/truck), and \textbf{Spurious-STL10} (the same corruption cue on $96{\times}96$
natural images, car vs.\ truck). In each, the majority training group has
spurious--label correlation $0.9$, the minority $0.5$, and the test set flips it
to $0.1$.

Table~\ref{tab:regimes} and Figure~\ref{fig:three} show the pattern is
consistent. Among no-group-label methods, the second-order residual variant is
best in every regime. On the hardest, most naturalistic regime (STL10) the
margins are largest and statistically clear (seed-paired Wilcoxon, 10 seeds):
$+28.1$~pp over vanilla ($p=0.002$), $+17.2$~pp over Ren2018 ($p=0.002$), and a
\textbf{second-order isolation} of $+12.9$~pp over the first-order term alone
($p=0.010$). The Colored-MNIST isolation ($+4.7$~pp, $p=0.033$) and
Corrupted-CIFAR isolation ($+4.9$~pp, $p=0.042$) reproduce the same effect.
Fairds-2 also has the lowest seed variance among no-group-label methods.

\subsection{The cross-term's signal is the orthogonal residual}
\label{sec:residual}

To test whether the residual $r$ is a real signal or noise, we build five arms
that share $\phi^{(1)}$ but treat $r$ differently: \emph{phi1} (first order only),
\emph{parallel} ($(1-\alpha\beta)\phi^{(1)}$, the shrinkage part),
\emph{residual-real} ($\phi^{(1)}-\alpha r$), \emph{residual-shuffle}
($r$ permuted within class$\times\phi^{(1)}$-quantile bins), and \emph{sign-flip}
($\phi^{(1)}+\alpha r$). Figure~\ref{fig:residual} reports the result.

The ordering is unambiguous: \textbf{real $>$ shuffle $>$ parallel}, and
sign-flip collapses. On Corrupted-CIFAR (20 seeds), residual-real beats
residual-shuffle by $+5.9$~pp ($p=0.0015$) and the parallel/shrinkage arm by
$+9.2$~pp ($p<10^{-4}$); sign-flipping the residual destroys accuracy
($0.07$, $p=5\times10^{-4}$). If $r$ were noise, shuffling it within matched
bins would not matter --- but it does. Thus the second-order benefit is a
real, sample-specific \emph{directional} signal, not global smoothing.

\caption{\textbf{Controlled probe.} Mean per-sample $\phi_i$ by group vs.\

On a controlled spurious-feature 2-layer MLP (a single strongly
label-correlated feature in the majority group), the per-sample $\phi_i$ of
Fairds-2 assign \emph{lower} value to majority than minority samples
(Mann--Whitney $U(\mathrm{maj}<\mathrm{min})$, $p=1.89\times10^{-8}$ at ratio
$0.9$), with $\Delta\phi(\mathrm{maj}-\mathrm{min})$ monotonically decreasing as
imbalance grows. This is the predicted mechanism: the cross-term penalizes
majority gradients aligned with the validation Hessian's dominant directions.

\subsection{Honest scope}
\label{sec:scope}
\paragraph{Stronger methods exist.}
JTT and GroupDRO reach higher worst-group accuracy in most regimes
(Table~\ref{tab:regimes}). Fairds-2's contribution is the closed-form,
single-pass cross-term mechanism, not peak accuracy.

\paragraph{A regime where it fails.}
On the canonical Waterbirds benchmark \citep{sagawa2020groupdro} with a
\emph{pretrained} ResNet-18, three Fairds variants (default EMA; warmup; per-step
no-EMA) all fail to match the corrected bi-level baseline (Ren2018 test\_worst
$\approx 0.49$ vs.\ Fairds-2 $\le 0.20$, $p<0.005$). With a strong pretrained
extractor, early per-sample gradients are small and noisy, so the EMA buffer
accumulates noise and reweighting destabilizes training. When the backbone is
frozen and Shapley acts only on a freshly initialized last layer (a DFR-style
setup), Fairds recovers to parity with the bi-level baseline and with last-layer
retraining --- but this is a boundary result, not a win.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}

Closed-form data valuation can shape training dynamics, but \emph{within a
characterized regime}. The second-order cross-term helps because of its
orthogonal residual --- a real, per-sample directional signal that down-weights
majority gradients aligned with the validation curvature --- and this mechanism
reproduces across color, texture, and natural-image spurious cues from scratch.
It does not transfer to pretrained fine-tuning, where bi-level meta-learning is
the better tool; the failure has a clear mechanistic cause (noisy small
gradients corrupting the EMA buffer). The method is not state-of-the-art:
two-stage (JTT) and group-label (GroupDRO) methods remain stronger, and the
second-order variant costs roughly $7.7\times$ vanilla wall-clock (the
first-order variant, $4.3\times$, is cheaper than the bi-level baseline). Its
value is conceptual: a single-pass, closed-form valuation whose curvature term
provably carries a usable robustness signal. Characterizing why the residual is
informative in some architectures but not others, and reducing the
second-order overhead, are the natural next steps.
